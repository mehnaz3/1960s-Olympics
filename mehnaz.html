A superior transportation system was built, which was integrated as a whole, and this allowed Tokyo the ability to meet short-term demands of the Olympics and long-term needs of growing population and traffic. This laid the foundation for Tokyo’s current prosperity. The Tokyo Olympics of 1964 were “a typical example of how mega-events serve as a ‘trigger for large-scale urban improvements’ since the 1960 Games Rome...Urban planners used the Games to improve the infrastructure of Tokyo, something badly needed due to the hasty reconstruction after 1945 and the enormous growth of the city during the rapid industrialization of the 1950s and 1960s” (Tagsold). Communication technologies were rapidly advancing in the 1960s, and in the 1964 Games, the relationship between television and the Olympics was forever changed. Japanese technological advancements enabled the broadcast of the Olympic sports and events to be viewed live via global satellite distribution. Despite IOC strict policies and attempts to keep commercialism out of the Opening Ceremonies, Japan managed “to symbolically broadcast its post-war national identity as a peaceful and technological nation to the world, and to remind national citizens of the role of a strong, central state planned economy in guaranteeing the continued economic prosperity and social stability of the nation.” An American television host said during the Opening Ceremony’s media coverage that the Japanese team and their country is a “happy nation” who has worked “very, very hard” to get ready for the games. <i><b>Media Portrayal</b></i> <img src="https://vault.si.com/.image/c_limit%2Ccs_srgb%2Cfl_progressive%2Ch_2000%2Cq_auto:good%2Cw_800/MTY5MDk1MDk2OTAzMDE4MjIz/42803---cover-image.jpg" alt="Tokyo 1964 Olympic Open <i>SI</i> Cover"> The <i>Sports Illustrated</i> issue of October 19, 1964 complemented the tireless efforts of the Japanese people, from the Emperor to the common workers. Japanese technology also allowed the Olympics sports to be timed down to the hundredth second for the first time. Furthermore, “Emphasizing the broader social value of such projects, almost all new construction and renovation projects were conducted by the Secretaría de Obras Públicas, a federal government ministry dedicated to public construction projects.” Similarly, Olympic villages that were built to house the event athletes were not developed with a short-term, one purpose plan. Elaborating upon the earlier mention of stadium building connections to urban revitalization, “frenetic construction took place in an area designated the Ciudad Universitaria (University City) on the southern outskirts of the city. Forming the nucleus of the National University, the site included many innovative designs, and a sporting arena that, in the 1960s, would be remodeled to form the centerpiece of the Olympic Games.” Designed by Ramírez Vásquez, the Estadio Azteca (Aztec Stadium) was inaugurated in May 1966, and “Conceived in 1960, this privately-financed project aimed to give Mexico City the most prestigious football stadium in the Americas. With a capacity of 100,000, it not only became a source of national pride, but also an intrinsic part of Mexico’s successful bid to win the competition to host the 1970 football World Cup. Outside of the stadium realm, another major project in Mexico City by Ramírez Vásquez was the National Anthropology Museum, which was “opened in 1964 to house artefacts from the nation’s rich pre-Columbian heritage. It, like the majority of the projects described above, was built in the south of the city among the more exclusive neighbourhoods.” These projects and developments with a regional hub of economic activity were a reflection of the growing confidence that the business sector had for Mexico City. Paired with this urban growth, a supporting expansion of Mexico City’s transportation infrastructure evolved. With the city’s population on the rise, the construction of new communication links was inevitable. Periférico Sur became a ring road that skirted the southern zone of the city, where most of the developments mentioned above were located. This road project was not only a “tacit recognition of the city’s growing congestion problems, but also of the city authorities’ determination to match economic developments with improvements in the infrastructure.” As another major transportation project, Mexico City’s metro system began to be developed and expanded in the early 1960s. The metro system’s routes “were designed to link districts on the limits of the city, with business centres and provincial transport points, the scheme also contributed to the economic needs of the city.” The socioeconomic progress and development created a fast rising middle class, which sparked a demand for suitable housing. (Citations by Zolov) [[1972 Munich|1972 Munich]]<b>MUNICH STADIUM BUILDING AND CULTURAL DISPLAY</b> In context to the Munich stadium as an architectural symbol, because of Germany’s unique challenge and looming shadow of its historical past, Munich’s strongest area of concentration or effort to show progress and modernity became stadium building. <i><b>Olympiastadion München by Frei Otto, Munich 1972</b></i> <img src="https://static.dezeen.com/uploads/2016/08/olympiastadion-munchen-frei-otto-munich-olympics-1972_dezeen_sq.jpg" alt="Munich Stadium Aerial View"> <i><b>One of the most celebrated projects by Frei Otto – who was posthumously awarded the Pritzker Prize in 2015 – is his Olympics park in Munich. Munich built a new stadium that was charged with its self-conceptualized meaning of identifying as a democratic state. “The Olympic stadium in Munich became a symbol for the German democratic state after the Second World War at the time of the Games.” Leading politicians played an important role in the Olympic Organizing Committee of Munich, and they specifically “designed the Munich Games as a contrast to the Olympic Games of 1936.” This committee “wanted to set a positive, West German counter-example to the communist regime in East Germany...The intention to present to the world a democratic, cosmopolitan and amicable Germany ran through the whole concept of the Olympic Games in Munich.” <i><b>Colorful and Refreshing Changes</b></i> Similar to the combined or fused nature of stadium building, urban revitalization, and cultural display aspects in the analysis of Mexico City’s Olympics, Munich’s stadium discussion is interlaced with the category of cultural display and image, along with urban revitalization. The stadium decor, style, and Opening Ceremony presentation was all mindful of emulating an open and democratic feel to the event. For example, the designer Otl Aicher created “a blaze of colours and pastels of the rainbow. The seats in the stadium, the clothes of the employees and the flag group in the upper meadow field were fashioned in this new colour design. The blue, green and yellow flags were perceived to be ‘refreshing’ instead of ‘sentimental’ or ‘nationalistic.’ In addition, the pacifist character was stressed by the fact that the military was not in attendance at the opening ceremony.” Similarly, stewards and policemen did not carry any obvious weapons and were in colorful clothing, instead of police uniforms. The Organizing Committee wanted to avoid any reference or association to the “old Germany” and hoped to “produce the picture of a peaceful Federal Republic.” <i><b>Distancing from the Past</b></i> As far as the actual construction and architecture of Munich’s stadium complex, as mentioned above with the cultural display, the main goal or purpose of the structure was to be set as far opposite as possible from 1936 Berlin. Munich did not have any facilities to accommodate a mega-sporting event such as the Olympics. A new sporting complex was built specifically for the 1972 Games, four kilometers outside the center of Munich. The organizers wanted to avoid a monumental structure like that of Berlin, which gave the impression of a colossal and oversized complex and seated more spectators that imagined. The sporting complex had many adjoining or clustered buildings that lied on an impressive grandiose, green corridor. The three main arenas were united under a tent-like, transparent roof, and “This unity also matched the original idea of understatement and was intended to inspire a cheerful atmosphere in its visitors." The roof connections resembled Gothic lines that characterized German architecture. In addition, “A lawn stretched around the sports buildings and a newly built lake was designed to contribute to the peaceful and friendly atmosphere intended by the organisers.” Daniel Brenner described in his September 1972, <i>America</i> article, “The remarkable designs for the Olympic landscape and buildings—from the cascading tiers of the competitor’s garden apartments to the bright yellow bubble-topped portable lavatories to the color-coded seats in the floodlit-for-TV oval stadium—turn the Olympiazentrum into science fiction.” Key phrases were carved into in the roof construction of the Munich Stadium, saying, “self-confident behavior,” and “attitude of abstinence.” The top structure was considered revolutionary for its time, and it became an iconic symbol of the Munich Olympics. “Steel cables, which were used for the first time on such a large scale, stabilized the large, sweeping canopies of acrylic glass. The construction was supposed to create an open and transparent atmosphere, which contrasted with the nation’s striving for power in the 1936 Games.” <i><b>Olympiapark München | Frei Otto</b></i> <iframe src="https://player.vimeo.com/video/123801877?color=ffffff&title=0&byline=0&portrait=0" width="740" height="372" frameborder="0" allow="autoplay; fullscreen" allowfullscreen></iframe> <a href="https://www.archdaily.com/615700/video-olympiapark-munchen-frei-otto"> More Munich Stadium Information</a> <i><b>Olympic Contradictions: Lighter and Gigantic</b></i> Although the roof was intended to be airy and to symbolize the opposite of power or dominance, “The Munich roof has to be understood as the self-confident statement of a modern Germany.” In detail, “The 74,000 square metres of the roof construction were a sign for the mastery of modern technology and economic effectiveness. The foundation stone of the Olympic Stadium...set new worldwide benchmarks: 123 base plates had to be made; pylons, each 81 metres tall, carried the stadium roof’s 3400 tons; the cables had to withstand a pressure of 4000 tons; and the length of the whole cable span was equal to that of several apartment buildings.” Therefore, the materials that built the roof were not light and airy, as was its intended image or symbolism, but they were “assembled in amazing static construction, which emphasised the contrast between the key attributes, ‘lightness’ and ‘lightheartedness.’” In essence, “Besides the interpretation of a new, democratic and optimistic Germany, the Munich architecture was supposed to symbolize a modern and powerful Federal Republic.” On the downside, the “light” roof and less monumental stadium complex also became a symbol of Olympic “gigantism,” and the cost was no less magnanimous. “The costs of the construction of the whole Olympic area were recalculated at a Figure 400-500% higher than the initial estimate. Some of the major examples of developments included “fabulous new sports facilities and parkland, six thousand apartments, 1,800 student flats, three schools with 7,650 places, and a large exhibition hall.” As in Tokyo and Mexico City, in order to support or link the new sites and developments, Munich’s transportation infrastructure was dramatically improved between 1965 and 1972. “Based on the German Federal Railway’s existing grid, a star-shaped suburban rail system (S-Bahn) was completed with twelve separate lines and 134 stops, stretching thirty-five kilometers from the historic center into the region.” <img src="https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f6/S-Bahn_Muenchen_Type_420.jpg/340px-S-Bahn_Muenchen_Type_420.jpg" alt="Munich S-Bahn"> A pedestrian zone was created due to tunnel lines east and west of the river being connected and the old part of the city to the new, with 4.2 kilometers of underground track. Individual car use was reduced by 40 percent, and the banning of traffic from the city center...restored the city’s historic luster.” Of course, as with most major expansions and developments, several drawbacks and urban imbalances existed, but in broader or collective terms, overall, “Munich became a more attractive city in which to live.” (Citations by Schiller and Young) [[Global Downturn and Olympic Hosting Decline|Global Downturn and Olympic Hosting Decline]] <b>CONCLUSION</b> The promise of Olympic hosting may have been at its last cusp during the rise of the 1960s decade. Furthermore, “Emphasizing the broader social value of such projects, almost all new construction and renovation projects were conducted by the Secretaría de Obras Públicas, a federal government ministry dedicated to public construction projects.” Similarly, Olympic villages that were built to house the event athletes were not developed with a short-term, one purpose plan. The housing was part of “a broader plan to extend the city’s urban development.” [[Mexico City Urban Revitalization|Mexico City Urban Revitalization]] <b>MEXICO CITY URBAN REVITALIZATION</b> Urban revitalization in preparation for hosting the Olympics was implemented much in advance to the Games, and since Mexico was already mindful of its desires to achieve modernity and progress, urban transformation in anticipation of hosting events or exhibiting Mexican advancements was well on its way prior to the Baden Baden decision for Mexico City to be the 1968 host. According to The International Journal of the History of Sport, “Prestigious landmark constructions across the city bore witness to the degree of confidence shared by the business sector.” For example, “In the early 1950s, the Auditorio Nacional on the edge of Chapultepec Park was inaugurated as an exhibition, concert, and conference centre. When it was opened in 1956, the Torre Latinoamericano in Mexico City’s business quarter became the latest succession of skyscrapers in the city, and for a time it was the tallest building in Latin America. Ramírez Vásquez, the Chairman of the Mexican Organizing Committee (MOC), felt that there was no better place to display Mexico’s progress and advancement than in the host city of Mexico City itself. Elaborating upon the earlier mention of stadium building connections to urban revitalization, “frenetic construction took place in an area designated the Ciudad Universitaria (University City) on the southern outskirts of the city. Forming the nucleus of the National University, the site included many innovative designs, and a sporting arena that, in the 1960s, would be remodeled to form the centerpiece of the Olympic Games.” Designed by Ramírez Vásquez, the Estadio Azteca (Aztec Stadium) was inaugurated in May 1966, and “Conceived in 1960, this privately-financed project aimed to give Mexico City the most prestigious football stadium in the Americas. With a capacity of 100,000, it not only became a source of national pride, but also an intrinsic part of Mexico’s successful bid to win the competition to host the 1970 football World Cup. Outside of the stadium realm, another major project in Mexico City by Ramírez Vásquez was the National Anthropology Museum, which was “opened in 1964 to house artefacts from the nation’s rich pre-Columbian heritage. It, like the majority of the projects described above, was built in the south of the city among the more exclusive neighbourhoods.” These projects and developments with a regional hub of economic activity were a reflection of the growing confidence that the business sector had for Mexico City. Paired with this urban growth, a supporting expansion of Mexico City’s transportation infrastructure evolved. With the city’s population on the rise, the construction of new communication links was inevitable. Periférico Sur became a ring road that skirted the southern zone of the city, where most of the developments mentioned above were located. This road project was not only a “tacit recognition of the city’s growing congestion problems, but also of the city authorities’ determination to match economic developments with improvements in the infrastructure.” As another major transportation project, Mexico City’s metro system began to be developed and expanded in the early 1960s. The metro system’s routes “were designed to link districts on the limits of the city, with business centres and provincial transport points, the scheme also contributed to the economic needs of the city.” The socioeconomic progress and development created a fast rising middle class, which sparked a demand for suitable housing. Following suit for progress and modernity, an ambitious new housing project was built, “Borrowing heavily from European designs for density, modern domestic complexes, the Nonoalco-Tlatelolco housing development of the early 1960s was geared to cater for the white collar sector.” [[Mexico City Cultural Display|Mexico City Cultural Display]] <b>MEXICO CITY CULTURAL DISPLAY</b> Mexico City’s strongest area of concentration or effort could be measured in its usage of cultural display for its Olympic message. The Mexican Organizing Committee (MOC), under Ramírez Vásquez launched a cultural program that was an innovative feat for an Olympic host city. This “ambitious, broad-based cultural and visual arts program came to be known as the Cultural Olympiad.” Vásquez’s goal with the yearlong cultural campaign was to garner greater domestic support and involvement for the Olympic effort, along with a mission to alleviate negative stereotypes and doubts from abroad. The effort that for the cultural venture was so in-depth and multi-faceted that it gained a reputation as being another Olympics of its own, called the “‘parallel Olympics.’” Mexico’s task as a host was in some ways more challenging than Japan and Germany, as Mexico typically existed under a negative or derogatory foreign stereotype as an underdeveloped, “‘disorganized South’” Global South nation. Japan and Germany had both shown and been global powers, regardless of negative postwar legacies and other racist or cultural stereotypes. Mexicans and their functionality was predominantly looked upon abroad as the “‘land of manaña’” one that was lazy, unmotivated, procrastinating, and bogged down or stuck in its past. Mexico had a reputation for being an underdeveloped and “backward” country who could not emerge or rise above its socioeconomic and political problems of the past. Much of these negative attitudes were ethno-racially charged by Western superiority, and therefore, it was important for Mexicans to showcase and prove their cultural worth and virtues. The Olympic Charter does mention or highlight the importance of a cultural component for the Olympic Games, but “Mexico was in fact the first host country to turn an emphasis on culture into an integral aspect of the Games themselves.” According to Professor Eric Zolov, “the Cultural Olympiad program involved twenty separate spheres of activities (mirroring the twenty athletic contests) and ranged from exhibitions of modern scientific advances, to a graphic arts program, film, dance, poetry, and theatre productions. There was also a ‘youth camp’ and an international sculpture competition.” Peace was a dominant goal or message for the cultural display, and five discursive components were entailed in the Cultural Olympiad. The first component entailed the usage of a peace dove as a symbolic icon, along with the depiction of Mexico as a “peacemaker” throughout the world. The second one involved “the elaboration and dissemination of an official logo that conveyed a cosmopolitan, ‘forward-looking’ sensibility via an Op-Art aesthetic.” The third component reached even further with a progressive cultural image of women, “which highlighted the presence of women in central aspects of Olympic promotion, thus reflecting both the ‘pretty face’ of Mexico but also its ‘modern’ values (i.e. emphasizing women’s upward social mobility). As part of this component, a Mexican “modern woman” was selected to light the Olympic cauldron during the Opening Ceremony. For Vásquez, the question of who would carry the torch into the stadium and light the cauldron was one of the most pressing matters for Mexico City’s Olympics. He was highly cognizant of following the grandeur and symbolism of the Japanese torchbearer who was born the same day as the atomic bomb dropping in Hiroshima. Writer Amy Bass was quoted as saying, “‘As the first woman to light the Olympic flame, the young farmer’s daughter presented a beautiful, non-commercial image that spoke well to the political times, as well as the heritage of the host country and its quest for modernity.’” The fourth area of the Cultural Olympiad was elaborately staged folkloric performances, which culminated with “the arrival of the Olympic flame at the pre-Hispanic site of Teotihuacan.” This advertised a diversity and inclusiveness, which highlighted “Mexico’s cultural ‘authenticity’ while underscoring the nation’s unique commitment to racial and ethnic harmony.” The final element of the Olympiad incorporated “the liberal use of bright colors in general, thus reinforcing a popular association with Mexico as a ‘festive and exotic land.’” These five components of Mexico’s Cultural Olympiad were the doubted host’s most outward exhibition of progress and modernity for the world stage. In summary, Zolov argued, “Together they comprised a vision of Mexico in which the nation was perceived as a land where, on the one hand, international traditions demonstrated profound tolerance of political difference while, on the other, indigenous cultural traditions were framed by and interfaced seamlessly with a forward-looking embrace of modern values.” Like Tokyo, Mexico City provided a strategic and calculated balance between the old and the new, or between cultural heritage and modernity, which sought an improved domestic national identity and an enhanced international or global recognition. [[1972 Munich|1972 Munich]]<b>MUNICH STADIUM BUILDING AND CULTURAL DISPLAY</b> In context to the Munich stadium as an architectural symbol, because of Germany’s unique challenge and looming shadow of its historical past, Munich’s strongest area of concentration or effort to show progress and modernity became stadium building. Modrey claimed that the Olympic Games serve a political function, and especially Olympic sports complexes in the latter part of the twentieth century were postwar political instruments. Modrey compared Italy and Germany as having fractured national identities because of their fascist and dictatorship pasts from World War II. Munich built a new stadium that was charged with its self-conceptualized meaning of identifying as a democratic state. Modrey argued, “the Olympic stadium in Munich became a symbol for the German democratic state after the Second World War at the time of the Games.” She explained that leading politicians played an important role in the Olympic Organizing Committee of Munich, and that they specifically “designed the Munich Games as a contrast to the Olympic Games of 1936.” This committee “wanted to set a positive, West German counter-example to the communist regime in East Germany…The intention to present to the world a democratic, cosmopolitan and amicable Germany ran through the whole concept of the Olympic Games in Munich.” Similar to the combined or fused nature of stadium building, urban revitalization, and cultural display aspects in the analysis of Mexico City’s Olympics, Munich’s stadium discussion is interlaced with the category of cultural display and image, along with urban revitalization. The stadium decor, style, and Opening Ceremony presentation was all mindful of emulating an open and democratic feel to the event. For example, the designer Otl Aicher created “a blaze of colours and pastels of the rainbow. The seats in the stadium, the clothes of the employees and the flag group in the upper meadow field were fashioned in this new colour design. The blue, green and yellow flags were perceived to be ‘refreshing’ instead of ‘sentimental’ or ‘nationalistic.’ In addition, the pacifist character was stressed by the fact that the military was not in attendance at the opening ceremony.” Similarly, stewards and policemen did not carry any obvious weapons and were in colorful clothing, instead of police uniforms. The Organizing Committee wanted to avoid any reference or association to the “old Germany” and hoped to “produce the picture of a peaceful Federal Republic.” As far as the actual construction and architecture of Munich’s stadium complex, as mentioned above with the cultural display, the main goal or purpose of the structure was to be set as far opposite as possible from 1936 Berlin. Munich did not have any facilities to accommodate a mega-sporting event such as the Olympics. A new sporting complex was built specifically for the 1972 Games, four kilometers outside the center of Munich. The organizers wanted to avoid a monumental structure like that of Berlin, which gave the impression of a colossal and oversized complex and seated more spectators that imagined. The sporting complex had many adjoining or clustered buildings that lied on an impressive grandiose, green corridor. The three main arenas were united under a tent-like, transparent roof, and Modrey said, “This unity also matched the original idea of understatement and was intended to inspire a cheerful atmosphere in its visitors. The roof connections resembled Gothic lines that characterized German architecture. In addition, “A lawn stretched around the sports buildings and a newly built lake was designed to contribute to the peaceful and friendly atmosphere intended by the organisers.” Daniel Brenner described in his September 1972, America article, “The remarkable designs for the Olympic landscape and buildings—from the cascading tiers of the competitor’s garden apartments to the bright yellow bubble-topped portable lavatories to the color-coded seats in the floodlit-for-TV oval stadium—turn the Olympiazentrum into science fiction.” Key phrases were carved into in the roof construction of the Munich Stadium, saying, “self-confident behavior,” and “attitude of abstinence.” The top structure was considered revolutionary for its time, and it became an iconic symbol of the Munich Olympics. Modrey elaborated, “Steel cables, which were used for the first time on such a large scale, stabilized the large, sweeping canopies of acrylic glass. The construction was supposed to create an open and transparent atmosphere, which contrasted with the nation’s striving for power in the 1936 Games.” Although the roof was intended to be airy and to symbolize the opposite of power or dominance, Modrey claimed that “the Munich roof has to be understood as the self-confident statement of a modern Germany.” She described that “The 74,000 square metres of the roof construction were a sign for the mastery of modern technology and economic effectiveness. The foundation stone of the Olympic Stadium…set new worldwide benchmarks: 123 base plates had to be made; pylons, each 81 metres tall, carried the stadium roof’s 3400 tons; the cables had to withstand a pressure of 4000 tons; and the length of the whole cable span was equal to that of several apartment buildings.” Therefore, the materials that built the roof were not light and airy, as was its intended image or symbolism, but they were “assembled in amazing static construction, which emphasised the contrast between the key attributes, ‘lightness’ and ‘lightheartedness.’” In essence, according to Modrey, “Besides the interpretation of a new, democratic and optimistic Germany, the Munich architecture was supposed to symbolize a modern and powerful Federal Republic.” On the downside, the “light” roof and less monumental stadium complex also became a symbol of Olympic “gigantism,” and the cost was no less magnanimous. It was reported that “The costs of the construction of the whole Olympic area were recalculated at a Figure 400-500% higher than the initial estimate. In the end, the Olympic construction costs totaled DM 1.35 billion,” which was largely due to “the difficult and expensive construction of the roof.” [[Munich Urban Revitalization|Munich Urban Revitalization]] <b>MUNICH URBAN REVITALIZATION</b> Urban revitalization for the 1972 Games created much improvement and enhancement for Munich, and in turn for West Germany. Some of the major examples of developments included “fabulous new sports facilities and parkland, six thousand apartments, 1,800 student flats, three schools with 7,650 places, and a large exhibition hall.” As in Tokyo and Mexico City, in order to support or link the new sites and developments, Munich’s transportation infrastructure was dramatically improved between 1965 and 1972. Authors Schiller and Young reported, “Based on the German Federal Railway’s existing grid, a star-shaped suburban rail system (S-Bahn) was completed with twelve separate lines and 134 stops, stretching thirty-five kilometers from the historic center into the region.” A pedestrian zone was created due to tunnel lines east and west of the river being connected and the old part of the city to the new, with 4.2 kilometers of underground track. In addition, “The city’s first two subway lines (U-Bahn) were also added, the first complementing S-Bahn with a twelve-kilometer north-south axis, the second providing a speedy link between Marienplatz and the Olympic venues four kilometers away.” Many roads were built in order to alleviate traffic congestion and to meet the simultaneous “sharp rise in car ownership.” These developments would have occurred eventually over time, but the advent of hosting of the Olympic Games “brought financial support and a sense of urgency.” Munich gained what the cities of “Frankfurt and Stuttgart took decades and much greater sums to achieve. Individual car use was reduced by 40 percent, and the banning of traffic from the city center…restored the city’s historic luster.” Of course, as with most major expansions and developments, several drawbacks and urban imbalances existed, but in broader or collective terms, overall, “Munich became a more attractive city in which to live.” [[Global Downturn and Olympic Hosting Decline|Global Downturn and Olympic Hosting Decline]] <b>CONCLUSION</b> The promise of Olympic hosting may have been at its last cusp during the rise of the 1960s decade. A recent Forbes article claimed that Olympic expenses have exceeded budget expectations since 1960. Forbes quoted a 2016 study by the Saïd Business School at the University of Oxford, “‘For a city and nation to decide to stage the Olympic Games is to decide to take on one of the most costly and financially most risky type of mega-project that exists, something that many cities and nations have learned to their peril.’” As examined through the case study comparisons, the Olympic Games of 1964 Tokyo, 1968 Mexico City, and 1972 Munich, demonstrated how nations were able to utilize Olympic hosting for their own progress and modernity. Unfortunately, the 1960s were perhaps a timely boom or prospect where the benefits could still be legitimized over the drawbacks.
